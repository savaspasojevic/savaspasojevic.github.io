(I). This section contains computer projects, all of which are intended for purely theoretical insights. Programs that have real-world applicability are typically not available for unauthorized viewership because a high degree of technical competence is required. I have previously made the mistake of discussing quantitative finance with persons that have no idea what questions to ask and what types of projects are undertaken in such a field. (II). Some of the projects here pertain to mathematical finance. A security, defined within a general framework, is not likely to be directly equivalent in its behavior to a security subject to real-world market conditions. The scientific words commonly used to describe this phenomenon are "variablility", "variance", and "volatility". The more familiar terminology uses words such as "risk", "uncertainty", and "liability".

(III). In mathematical finance, some fundamental characterizations of uncertainty are theoretical in nature. Projects here are designed to analyze this mathematical uncertainty and have no guarantee of providing a useful framework for characterizing real-world uncertainty. Once this acknowledgement is made, there will be no confusion regarding project classification according to the two categories, theoretical and applicable.   • 4. Option Greeks (May 2020)

    • Plotting of Option Greeks and Verification of Formulas

      • In this project I plotted option greeks and checked whether my results were consist with those from chapter 3 of the book "Financial Engineering Advanced Background Series" by Dan Stefanica. The option greeks are as follows; let \(V\) be the value of a porfolio of derivative securities on one underlying asset. Then

    • 1. Delta \(\Delta(V) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\)
    • 2. Gamma \(\Gamma(V) = \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial S^2}\)
    • 3. Theta \(\Theta(V) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial t}\)
    • 4. Rho \(\rho(V) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\)
    • 5. Vega \(v(V) = \frac{\partial V}{\partial \sigma}\)
